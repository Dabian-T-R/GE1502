LASER CHESS / MAZE

A 2-player laser strategy board game designed for 5th graders at Melrose Leadership Academy — combining optics, spatial reasoning, and STEM education.

Team Members

01 — Project Overview

Problem Statement

Abstract

Our team designed and built a laser-based STEM board game for fifth-grade students at Melrose Leadership Academy. We started by talking with students and figuring out what actually keeps them interested in class — they liked hands-on activities more than passive learning, competition, movement, and games that felt fun right away. They also wanted something that could be explained fast and played within a normal class period.

The final design is a laser-based game built around a board, removable pieces, mirrors, blockers, a laser emitter, a receiver, and RP2040-based electronics. Getting there took sketches, concept discussions, early prototypes, CAD work, electronics integration, testing, and redesign — including a late-stage board warp that forced a design change. The final prototype demonstrates a real, interactive STEM game reflecting student feedback, real engineering constraints, and repeated problem-solving.

The Challenge

Fifth-grade students at Melrose Leadership Academy need inclusive, engaging STEM games that provide meaningful early exposure to STEM concepts while supporting participation from all children — regardless of ability, gender, or language background.

Many existing STEM games are insufficiently aligned with California Common Core standards and fail to address gender disparities in STEM identity development. The challenge was to design an interactive game that is developmentally appropriate, curriculum-aligned, and intentionally inclusive.

The Solution — Laser Grid

A tabletop laser-puzzle game (DOM-K) on a 6×6 grid with interchangeable physical pieces and an electronics system that detects piece placement, controls a laser emitter, and registers beam hits. Supports single-player puzzle mode (challenge cards) and two-player competitive mode.

Physical Board — Final Construction

The playing surface is a flat 6×6 grid of square cells. The base is a three-layer system: a thick plywood base (chosen for straightness) with embedded magnets, a laser-cut wood grid drilled onto the base, and a thin painted plywood top serving as the decorative playing surface. The board itself has no exposed electronics.

3-layer plywood system solved board warping issues
Laser-cut wood grid drilled to base
Board dimensions: 12×12×1.36 inches
No exposed electronics on playing surface

Six Game Piece Types

Each piece is sized to fit within a single 6×6 grid cell. Embedded board magnets interact with reed switches inside the laser and king pieces.

Laser Piece

The beam source. Contains a laser diode, a battery, and a reed switch. Placing it on a grid cell closes the reed switch via the board's embedded magnet, powering the diode.

King Piece

The target. A photoreceptor on the front face detects incident laser light. An onboard RF transmitter sends a hit signal to the external electronics unit.

Deflector Piece

A one-sided 45° mirror on a square base. Redirects an incident beam by 90°. The reflective side is marked visually on top; the opposite side absorbs the beam.

Diagonal Piece

A 22.5° mirror on a square base. Redirects an incident beam by 45°, converting a cardinal-direction beam into a diagonal beam (or vice versa).

Shield Piece

An opaque block that absorbs the laser beam on all four faces. It does not deflect or transmit light in any direction.

Barricade Piece

A low-profile square that sits flush to the board surface. Short enough that a laser beam travels over it unobstructed, but blocks other pieces from that cell.

Magnet & Reed Switch Activation System

The key innovation: magnets are embedded in the board base at each grid cell. The laser piece and king piece each contain a reed switch. When placed on a cell, the board magnet closes the reed switch inside the piece, activating the laser diode or photoreceptor — no exposed wiring on the playing surface.

This replaced an earlier copper-tape contact rail design, improving safety, simplifying construction, and making the system more robust overall.

Board magnets embedded at each grid cell
Reed switches inside laser & king pieces close on placement
King piece uses RF transmitter to signal hits wirelessly
No exposed conductors on playing surface — child safe

Electronics Unit

All electronics are housed in a separate unit connected wirelessly via the king piece's RF link. Built around a Raspberry Pi Pico (RP2040) running MicroPython.

User Controls

  • • Power switch (back) — on/off
  • • 2 front buttons — game mode select (single / two-player)
  • • 2 top buttons — chess-clock style, one per player

Internal Peripherals

  • • 16×2 I²C LCD display — all text feedback
  • • RF receiver module — receives hit signals from king piece
  • • 4 GPIO-connected user buttons
  • • Status LED (green = win, red = loss)

Game Logic Flowchart

Single-Player Mode

1 Power ON → LCD: "Welcome! Choose game mode"
2 Mode selected → LCD: "Get Ready!" → stopwatch starts
3 Player arranges deflectors/shields/barricades on grid
4 Pico monitors RF receiver continuously for hit signal
W Hit registered → stopwatch stops → LED green → "You Win!"

Two-Player Mode

S Each player gets: laser piece, king, symmetric piece complement
T Top button = chess clock — pressing ends your turn, starts opponent's
P Both lasers fire simultaneously at all times
W Hit registered → win credited to player whose clock is running

09 — Discussion & Analysis

Design Evaluation

Board Flatness Challenge

Board flatness was one of the most critical design constraints — even a small angular deviation would cause the laser beam to miss its intended path. Early prototypes suffered from warping, making the laser unreliable.

The solution came from rethinking the board as a three-layer system: thick plywood at the base (chosen for straightness), a 3D-printed middle layer housing the embedded magnets, and a thin painted plywood top as the decorative playing surface.

Key Design Pivot: Copper → Magnets

The original plan used copper wires in the board to transfer power and data. This introduced safety and reliability concerns — exposed electrical contacts on a toy for young children posed a clear risk.

Switching to reed switches and embedded magnets eliminated all exposed conductors on the playing surface. Wireless RF communication handled the rest, improving safety, simplifying construction, and making the system more robust.

Safety Evaluation

Safety was a primary constraint given the target age group (5–10). The shift from copper wires removed all exposed electrical contacts. The layered board construction kept all internal components enclosed. Pieces were designed with no sharp edges.

Minor areas worth noting: ensuring laser power level is formally certified appropriate for young users, and confirming pieces are durable enough for rough handling.

Component-Level Testing

Component-level testing proved essential. The team tested each subsystem independently before integration: wireless transmission, laser straightness, magnet fitment, laser housing fitment, and piece fitment into the board.

The LCD screen failed spontaneously during integration, reinforcing the importance of testing displays independently before full assembly.

Overall Assessment

The prototype demonstrated that the concept works. Both competitive and puzzle modes are playable, the wireless system functions, the timer box tracks gameplay accurately, and the board maintains the flatness needed for reliable laser travel. The project came in under $100, meeting the cost constraint.

The core gameplay experience worked as designed, and the prototype successfully demonstrated a laser-based strategy game that is both engaging and safe for its intended users.

10 — Conclusion

Summary

DOM-K: Mission Accomplished

The final design is a two-player laser strategy game targeted at children ages 5 to 10. Each player moves pieces and adjusts mirrors on a shared 6×6 board, attempting to redirect their laser to hit the opponent's king piece. The electronics unit detects hits, tracks per-player chess-clock time, and handles both game modes wirelessly.

The design met the majority of established goals and constraints. The budget was met, and the final product functioned as intended during testing. The shift from exposed copper wires to magnetic reed switches directly addressed the primary safety concern.

Despite challenges with laser housing alignment and board warping — both addressed iteratively — the core gameplay experience worked as designed.

Under $100

Project came in within the team budget. Majority of components sourced from SparkFun kit or borrowed from the makerspace.

Both Modes Delivered

Single-player puzzle mode with 10 challenge cards, and two-player competitive mode with chess-clock timing.

Kids Loved It

MLA 5th graders responded overwhelmingly positively. Clear visual feedback and automatic activation made the game immediately intuitive.

11 — Recommendations

Next Steps for DOM-K

Four priority recommendations for the next stage of development or future teams working on a similar system.

1

Expand & Formalize Student Playtesting

The MLA campus testing session was a strong start, but future evaluation should be longer and more structured.

  • Implement a formal pre/post concept check
  • Timed setup and play observations
  • Collect feedback in both English and Spanish to reach all MLA students
2

Simplify Two-Player Mode

Student feedback specifically identified two-player mode as confusing for the target age group.

  • Revise two-player instruction card with clearer step-by-step layout
  • Add visual diagrams showing board setup for each player
  • Consider color-coding player sides more prominently
3

Expand Challenge Card Deck & Difficulty

One student explicitly requested a bigger maze — the current difficulty ceiling is not high enough for all players.

  • Production version requires minimum 30 laminated cards
  • Span beginner, intermediate, and expert difficulty tiers
  • More complex configurations: longer beam paths, more pieces, tighter angles
4

Implement Automatic Game Reset

Currently the player must press a button to restart after each round, requiring adult involvement in a classroom setting.

  • Add automatic reset approximately 5 seconds after win/loss screen
  • Allows students to move directly into next attempt without help
  • Substantially improves pace during 15-minute classroom sessions
